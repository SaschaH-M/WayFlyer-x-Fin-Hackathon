The Problem

Pretty Fly is buying the right products in the wrong quantities.

The inventory isn't broken at the product level — it's broken at the variant level.

The data reveals a counterintuitive pattern: 19 products have some variants that are massively overstocked while other variants of the same product are completely sold out. This isn't a merchandising failure — it's a buying intelligence failure.

Pretty Fly's team is placing POs without visibility into which specific sizes and colors are actually selling. The result: £186K of cash buried in variants nobody wants, while £124K/month in demand goes unfulfilled because the right variants are at zero.

The contradiction: 69 variants have over 12 months of stock cover (£186K at cost). Simultaneously, 301 variants that sold in the last 3 months are now at zero inventory. The business is losing £124K/month in revenue from products it already knows how to sell — it just bought the wrong sizes.

The Build

Working Capital Intelligence Agent

An AI-powered conversational tool that tells Pretty Fly's operators exactly what to buy and what to discount — with direct £-impact.

The agent is grounded in six data tables — variants, inventory_movements, line_items, orders, po_line_items, and purchase_orders — and answers operational questions with specific, data-backed recommendations.

Why AI? The relationships between sales velocity, stock levels, lead times, and cash position are too multi-dimensional for a static dashboard. An operator needs to ask follow-up questions: "What if I only reorder the top 10?" "Which supplier can deliver fastest?" "What's my cash position if I place all 3 POs this month?" An LLM agent grounded in the data can answer these dynamically.

1. Reorder recommendations: For any product or variant, the agent computes current stock, sales velocity, seasonality, and landed cost — then recommends exact reorder quantities with a £-impact estimate.

2. Markdown identification: Flags variants with >12 months of cover and calculates the cash that would be freed by liquidating at various discount levels (cost recovery, 50% margin, etc.).

3. Cash impact forecasting: Given a proposed PO schedule, models the business's cash position month by month — surfacing exactly when cash runs negative and how much bridge capital would be needed.

4. Supplier optimization: Compares lead times across suppliers (Portugal, Italy, Turkey) and factors in payment terms to recommend which supplier to use for urgent reorders.

1. Seasonality risk: Restocking winter-weight variants in June may not yield immediate returns. The agent should factor in seasonal demand curves.

2. Cannibalization: If a customer wanted a Heavyweight Hoodie in Black/Large (stockout) and bought a Relaxed Hoodie instead (overstock), restocking may shift revenue between products rather than add new revenue. The £8.04× ratio assumes additive demand.

3. Lead time reality: Supplier lead times average 71 days. Revenue recovery isn't instant — the agent should model a 2-3 month lag between PO placement and revenue realization.

Mitigations in the Build

For seasonality: The agent computes a 12-month rolling average and flags seasonal patterns. Recommendations account for which variants are in their peak selling window.

For cannibalization: The agent can query whether substitute products exist in stock and flag potential substitution effects. The demo acknowledges this as a known limitation and offers a conservative (50%) discount on the projected uplift.

For lead times: The cash forecast explicitly models the 71-day gap between PO placement and revenue generation. The demo shows exactly when cash goes negative during the waiting period.

Even at 50% of projected uplift: the return is still £4.02 per £1 deployed — a 4× return that beats any ad campaign in the portfolio and carries zero platform risk.
